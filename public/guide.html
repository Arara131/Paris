Before You Leave Home

Our single best piece of advice

Pack light. You will thank us the moment you navigate your first cobblestone street. But also bring a collapsible duffel bag — empty, folded flat at the bottom of your case.

It is essentially impossible to leave Paris without acquiring things. The city is designed to seduce you into extra luggage — the markets, the wine shops, the patisserie boxes you will want to carry home. The empty duffel is your insurance policy against an impossible choice at the airport.

City & country hopping

The apartment's location makes it an exceptional base not just for Paris but for all of Europe. London is 2h15 by Eurostar, Brussels 1h22, Amsterdam 3h20. Rome, Barcelona, Lisbon — all a short flight. The empty duffel doubles as your weekend away bag.

I

Arrival

From airport to apartment · Step by step

At the airport
1

Skip the touts, find the taxi rank

Walk straight past anyone approaching you inside the terminal — illegal taxi peddlers are persistent and will cost you significantly more. Follow signs to the official Paris Taxis rank outside. Fares from CDG are fixed by law: approximately €56 to central Paris on the Right Bank, €65 to the Left Bank.

Ask the driver upfront whether they take cards. Some will claim their machine is broken on arrival — it rarely is. If paying cash for excess luggage, expect to negotiate; €2–5 per extra bag is standard.

2

The journey in

Around 30 minutes with clear roads, 45–60 minutes in normal traffic. Official taxis can use the bus lane, which meaningfully cuts journey time during rush hour. If you need a taxi pre-arranged, let us know — we can arrange transportation as part of your package at no extra cost.

When leaving for the airport, ask your driver to drop at your departure terminal's arrivals level (e.g. Terminal 2E arrivals). You then take your luggage up the escalators directly to check-in — far easier than navigating departures with bags.

3

Collect your keys — Hôtel Jeff

Your keys are held at Hôtel Jeff, 24 hours a day, for your convenience. Hôtel Jeff is in the 9th arrondissement — conveniently between the apartment and both the airport and the train stations. Ask your taxi to make a brief stop, or collect on foot: it is a 10-minute walk from the apartment building.

A note on keys. Paris is not like home in this regard. Keys here are cut against a registered security card, and replacing a lost key takes several days and involves bureaucracy. Lost keys carry a replacement fee of €400 — not to be punitive, but to reflect the genuine inconvenience and potential lost rental days. Please treat the key as you would a passport.

Finding the apartment

This is where it gets interesting — and once you know it, you will feel like a genuine Parisian with a proper secret address. Walk along rue Montmartre heading north from Boulevard Montmartre. Cross into the 9th arrondissement — the boundary is Boulevard Montmartre — and we are approximately half a block along on the right-hand side. Look for number 157, between a Picto store and a sandwicherie. You will see 159 on the building — however the entrance itself is accessed via 157, so walk through that door rather than looking for a separate entrance at 159.

1

The front door — 157

The entrance code is 23B59#. During weekdays between 8am and 5pm, buildings like ours that house both businesses and residences can often be entered simply by pressing the entry button — no code needed. After hours and at weekends, use the code. You also have a fob which will open the door instantly.

When leaving, press the button marked Porte to release the latch before pushing the door open. All doors auto-lock — never step out without your keys.

2

Through the hallway

There is a light button just inside — press it. Walk straight down the hallway. The first set of doors on your left belongs to number 157 — a different building entirely. Walk past them.

3

The lift

The lift is ahead of you. Call it with the button. A word on European lifts: this one is authentically Parisian in scale — two adults and a small child, or two adults with one large suitcase. Plan to make two trips if you are a group with luggage. It is not a failing of the building; it is a feature of a city built before the age of the wheeled suitcase.

4

The courtyard entrance — 159

Past the lift, follow the passage to the left into the mailbox area. Immediately to your right is a door to the courtyard. Step through and walk diagonally to the far right corner. This is the second entrance — the one that is truly yours.

The courtyard is the moment the city disappears. Even though the streets outside are alive at any hour, in here there is only quiet. This is part of what makes the apartment special.

5

Opening the courtyard door

Find the code box. Insert the key but do not push it all the way in — leave a small amount of key visible. Then jiggle and wiggle gently until you hear a soft magnetic pop. That is the door releasing. It opens inward toward you.

6

The stairs to the 4th floor

The steps here are original to the building — beautiful, worn, and slightly irregular in height. Take them carefully, especially with bags. France counts from the ground floor as zero (rez-de-chaussée), so the 4th floor by French count is what would be the 5th floor at home. Worth knowing before your legs complain.

If you are doing this on foot rather than lift: this is a genuine 19th-century Parisian staircase. It is a little like climbing history. Embrace it.

7

Your front door

Look for the door marked Gathua / Tang. Insert the key and turn it twice — this is a double lock. Once inside, you have arrived. Put down your bags. Notice how quiet it is.

II

The Apartment

116m² · 2 bedrooms · 2 bathrooms · 4th floor

The building at 159 rue Montmartre dates from around 1850 — a handsome Haussmann-era structure in one of Paris's historic commercial hearts. Stepping inside the apartment is a small architectural surprise: old world shell, entirely modern interior.

On your way in, notice the entrance hall downstairs. The chasse-roues — those rounded stone guards set into the wall at carriage height — are original to the building. They were placed there to protect the stonework from horse-drawn carriage wheels. They have been doing their job for over 170 years and show no signs of stopping.

The apartment itself sits on the 4th floor. While upper floors in Haussmann buildings typically have more modest ceiling heights than the grander lower levels, the space feels genuinely airy — the parquet floors, the considered proportions, and most of all the windows make it feel larger than it measures. Notice the original fireplace and its antique cast-iron fireback — a classic French feature that once protected the wall while radiating extra warmth back into the room. It no longer burns, but it anchors the living space beautifully.

The crosswind

Open the windows in both the TV room and the living room simultaneously. What follows is one of the apartment's quiet pleasures — a remarkable crosswind moves through the space, particularly in the warmer months. Paris summers are short and the apartment handles them without air conditioning almost entirely through this natural ventilation.

Things worth knowing

The windows

Each window has a dual mechanism — you can open just the top section for ventilation, or swing the whole panel open fully. When closing and locking, ensure the window sits completely flush before turning the lock. This matters in rain and in winter to keep the heat in.

Heating & cooling

The apartment has underfloor heating throughout — the warmth rises evenly and gently rather than blasting from a single point. It keeps the space consistently comfortable without ever feeling hot. Air conditioning is available for summer, though Paris heat typically lasts only a few weeks and the crosswind usually does the job beautifully.

The lamps

The paper lamps around the apartment are delicate — they puncture easily and are not easily replaced. Please handle with care and keep them away from small children. The distinctive architectural lamp in the living area was designed by the owner, inspired by a piece in an architecture magazine. It is one of a kind and genuinely irreplaceable.

The TV room

The TV room contains two sofa beds — fully functional as sleeping spaces for additional guests or for a lazy afternoon with a film and the windows open to the courtyard. The room works as both a media space and a second sitting area depending on how you want to use the apartment.

Welcome provisions

You will find two bottles of wine and a bottle of champagne waiting for you. These are yours — enjoy them whenever and however you like. If you open something and love it, feel free to leave something in return for the next guests. It has become a small ritual of the apartment and we love it.

The breakfast nook

Coffee is set up and ready. Cups are in the kitchen cabinets above. There is ample storage throughout — you are 116m² of Parisian real estate, not a hotel room. Use the space. Make yourself at home. That is the entire point.

Mid-stay cleaning

For longer stays, we offer a bi-weekly cleaning service at €100 per session — your space refreshed, linens changed, supplies restocked. We schedule this in advance so you can plan to be out for a few hours. Ask us to arrange it when you book.

III

The Passages Couverts

Les passages couverts · Your hidden city

The covered passages of Paris are one of the city's greatest secrets — built in the early 19th century as the world's first shopping arcades, forgotten by Haussmann's redesign, preserved in a state of amber-lit, dignified sleep. One entrance is almost your front door.

Passage des Panoramas
Le plus ancien passage de Paris · 1799
151 Rue Montmartre  ·  Daily 6am–midnight

The oldest surviving covered passage in Paris — and almost literally your front door. Opened in 1799, it was the first public space in France to receive gas lighting, in 1816. Enter through the heavy wooden doors into a long amber corridor of antique shops, stamp dealers, coin collectors, and bistros unchanged from the Belle Époque. The passage connects to three further galleries — Galerie Saint-Marc, Galerie Feydeau, Galerie Montmartre — covering an entire city block. Get intentionally lost.

Weekday afternoons when the dealers are in their element. Evening is magical — the old gas-style lamps are lit and the passage feels like a stage set for a film that hasn't been made yet.

Galerie Vivienne
Le passage le plus élégant · 1826
6 Rue Vivienne  ·  Daily 8:30am–8:30pm

Declared a historic monument in 1974 and widely considered the most beautiful passage in Paris. The hand-laid mosaic floors, the soaring glass roof, the neoclassical rotunda — it is the kind of space that stops conversation. Inside: Legrand Filles et Fils (one of the great wine shops of Paris), the ancient Librairie Jousseaume, and Bistrot Vivienne for coffee.

Enter via Rue des Petits-Champs — you arrive at the rotunda end and the whole gallery unfolds before you.

Passage Jouffroy & Passage Verdeau
La suite · 1847
10–12 Boulevard Montmartre

Cross Boulevard Montmartre from the Panoramas and continue your 19th-century walk. Jouffroy was the first passage built entirely of iron and glass — architecturally radical for its time. Verdeau beyond it is quieter still, a collector's world of old photographs, rare books and objects that exist nowhere else. All three chain together into one seamless walk without once returning to the street.

IV

Rue Montorgueil & the Market Quarter

Le marché du quartier · Your morning ritual

The last great market street in central Paris. Fish merchants, fromageries, boulangeries, wine merchants and florists trading shoulder to shoulder since 1645. Go early on a Sunday and you will understand exactly why we fell in love with this neighbourhood.

Stohrer
La plus ancienne pâtisserie de Paris · Est. 1730
51 Rue Montorgueil

Founded by Nicolas Stohrer, pastry chef to Louis XV, and operating from the same address for nearly three centuries. The rum baba was essentially invented here. The original painted ceiling and antique showcases are as intact as the recipes. Arrive before 9am.

Buy something to eat standing on the street. That is the right way to do it.

Frenchie
5 Rue du Nil  ·  Reservations essential

Chef Grégory Marchand's Michelin-starred restaurant, one street north of Montorgueil, that almost single-handedly transformed this neighbourhood into a serious dining destination. Market-driven and technically precise. The tasting menu is the move.

Frenchie Bar à Vins next door — no reservations, same kitchen philosophy, wine bar format. Arrive at 7pm or accept a wait.

L'Escargot Montorgueil
38 Rue Montorgueil  ·  Est. 1832

Nearly two centuries on the same street, the interior a listed monument, the dining room a Belle Époque stage set that has received Proust, Picasso and Dalí. The escargots six ways are as good as they ever were. Lunch is more affordable; the experience is identical.

Cul de Cochon
98 Rue Montorgueil

The finest charcuterie on the street. Cured beef, pork, pâtés, rillettes. If you are cooking or assembling dinner in the apartment, this is your first stop.

Legrand Filles et Fils
Le caviste de référence
1 Rue de la Banque  ·  Inside Galerie Vivienne

Over 3,000 labels and staff who genuinely know what they are talking about. Tell them what you are eating, what mood you are in, what you want to spend. They will find something remarkable. The small wine bar inside lets you drink from the same cellar.

VI

Culture, Architecture & Wandering

La ville lumière · The city itself

Everything feels like it is just 20 minutes away on foot — because it mostly is. The Louvre, the Marais, Notre-Dame, the Palais Royal. Walk without a plan. The city will take you somewhere worth being.

Place des Victoires
8 minutes on foot

A grand circular place centred on Louis XIV's equestrian statue, built in 1686. One of the finest proportioned urban spaces in Paris and almost entirely overlooked by visitors. The 17th-century buildings that ring it are extraordinarily preserved. Sit on the steps.

Palais Royal & Gardens
Le jardin secret
15 minutes on foot

The enclosed garden of the former royal palace — one of the most beautiful public spaces in Paris, almost entirely unknown to tourists. The arcaded walkways unchanged for 200 years. The Buren columns in the courtyard. The linden trees.

The finest picnic location in central Paris. Buy from Montorgueil, eat here.

Notre-Dame de Paris
20 minutes on foot

Reopened December 2024 after the 2019 fire. The restoration is extraordinary — the interior more luminous now than it has been in living memory. Go in the morning. Book timed entry online in advance (free, but queues without a reservation are long).

Le Marais
10–15 minutes on foot heading east

Paris's historic Jewish quarter, the Place des Vosges (1612), the Musée Picasso, the Musée Carnavalet. And the best falafel in Europe on Rue des Rosiers.

Avoid Saturdays. Sunday morning is the Marais at its most alive — uniquely open when the rest of Paris is still sleeping.

The Louvre
20 minutes on foot

Do not try to see it. Pick two or three rooms and give them the time they deserve. The Egyptian Antiquities rooms are extraordinary and rarely crowded. The Jardin des Tuileries beside it is one of the great formal gardens in France — free, 20 minutes, worth more than most paid attractions.

Evenings

La vie nocturne · The art of the long evening

Paris evenings are long, unhurried, and structured around pleasure. The aperitif before dinner. The dinner itself. The walk home past midnight. And if you need a crêpe at 12:30am — and you will — the crêperie is just a few steps from the door.

Frenchie Bar à Vins
6 Rue du Nil  ·  No reservations

Outstanding wine list. Small plates from the same kitchen as the Michelin-starred restaurant next door. No reservations — arrive early or accept a wait at the bar, which is not a hardship.

7pm when it opens. It fills in 30 minutes.

Legrand Filles et Fils
Wine bar inside Galerie Vivienne
1 Rue de la Banque

Drinking by the glass from one of the great cellars in Paris, inside a 19th-century passage as the evening empties it of visitors. There is genuinely no equivalent of this experience anywhere else.

Au Rocher de Cancale
78 Rue Montorgueil

Oysters, white wine, a Belle Époque interior, late into the evening. Originating as an oyster merchant's on what was once Paris's fish market street. The atmosphere alone is worth it.

The Midnight Crêperie
Just steps from the apartment

After a long evening of walking, drinking, eating — the city still alive around you — there is something specifically perfect about a late-night crêpe just a few steps from home. This is one of our favourite things about the apartment's location. You never have to choose between staying out and getting back.

Canal Saint-Martin & Oberkampf
Pour une soirée plus animée
20 minutes on foot or 10 by Vélib'

When you want a livelier evening: Canal Saint-Martin (10th) for canal-side bars in warm weather, Oberkampf and Bastille (11th) for the best bar concentration in Paris. Neither is tourist territory — this is where young Parisians actually spend their evenings.

Practical Notes

Quelques conseils · A few things worth knowing

Getting around

Paris is intentionally becoming a walking city — fewer cars, more pedestrian space, more terraces, more green. You will walk. A lot. Plan for it, enjoy it, and get good shoes. Vélib' (bike-share) is excellent for medium distances. Download the app. Nearest métro: Sentier (Line 3), Bourse (Line 3), Grands Boulevards (Lines 8 & 9).

Money & tipping

Service is included by law in all restaurant bills (service compris). Leaving a few euros after a good dinner is appreciated but entirely optional. At cafés, rounding up is sufficient. Nobody expects American-style tipping.

Restaurants & reservations

Dinner service starts at 7:30pm, rooms fill between 8 and 9pm. Book ahead for anything serious — a week for good bistros, three to four weeks for destination restaurants. Lunch is almost always more available and frequently better value, often the same kitchen at two-thirds the price.

Language

Four words open all doors: Bonjour (hello), s'il vous plaît (please), merci (thank you), excusez-moi (excuse me). Always begin every interaction with Bonjour. It is not optional — it is the social contract. Parisians who seem reserved will almost always warm up immediately when greeted properly.

Sundays

Many shops close. Montorgueil is at its most magnificent. The Marais is uniquely alive — its Jewish heritage means it operates when the rest of Paris doesn't. Treat Sunday as the city's best slow day and plan accordingly.

August

Paris goes on holiday. Some restaurants close for the month. The city is quieter, warmer, and in some ways more purely itself — tourists and locals coexisting on the terraces, the passages uncrowded, the pace slower even than usual. Embrace it.
